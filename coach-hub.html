Head vs Assistant Coach Expectations

We are the best women’s wrestling staff in the state and one of the best in the country. Our girls are lucky to have such a good and dedicated staff.

Head Coach Expectations

  • Create practice plans
  • Take care of or delegate whatever needs to be done — schedule, weights, Track, roster, Matboss, or anything else that needs to be done
  • Put assistants in a position to succeed
  • Make the final decisions

Assistant Coach Expectations

  • Assist in practice and meets and complete any delegated tasks
    • There is a checklist to go through in the preseason to make roles clear
    • Go to Coach Carl first with questions/concerns regarding anything about the program
  • Direct parents, wrestlers, and other coaches to Coach Carl if they have a concern

Leadership Keys

The leadership skill set is based on our ability to execute two fundamental aspects of leadership: 1) building trust, and 2) getting results with our wrestlers and guiding them to their full potential.

Building Trust Keys

1) Connection

  • Listen to the girls and value their feedback — one of the best ways to show them we care, even if we don’t use the feedback. (There is also a limit to how much we take in, so they don’t feel like they can run or overrule things.)
  • Connect with wrestlers on a personal level. Be positive. Get to know them. Create lollipop moments for them. Reach out to them when we don’t see them. Ask how they are doing, and talk to every girl every day.
  • Use resources to help the girls with things off the mat (Coach Beyond Training).

2) Character

  • Display ethical character and trust. Follow our pillars.

3) Competence

  • Have the competence, knowledge, and skill to help them. Use resources such as email newsletters.
  • Know why — point of emphasis this year. As head coach, Coach Carl needs to explain more of the why behind how we do things. There is now a “why” section to our practice plan, and we will go over a practice goal for each day. Coach Carl expects all assistants to know why if asked by the girls.

Getting Results Keys

1) Clarity

  • We are all on the same page. Know all information sent out in emails. Turn on SportsYou phone notifications.

2) Accountability

  • Provide accountability for our wrestlers so that they do what they say they will do. We set a good example by being accountable ourselves.

3) Expect and navigate adversity

  • Things will go wrong sometimes. We don’t flinch — we handle it. Have an elite mindset and follow the mindset practical applications.
Cain, Brian. (2019). Mental Performance Mastery Coaching Certification Course Manual (2nd ed.). Brian Cain Peak Performance.
04

Learning vs. Performance Practice

Not every practice should look or feel the same. Most of our practice time should be learning practice, with performance practice reserved for specific moments close to competition.

Learning Practice

  • The goal is long-term skill development, not looking good today.
  • Wrestlers are allowed a higher degree of error — mistakes are part of the process, not something to eliminate immediately.
  • Lower pressure, lower stakes. This is where risk-taking, experimentation, and trying new moves should happen.
  • Feedback is often delayed or given in summary form so wrestlers build their own internal feedback instead of relying on us for every rep.
  • This should make up the majority of our practices.

Performance Practice

  • The goal is to simulate competition — pace, pressure, and intensity should be close to match speed.
  • Wrestlers are held to a lower degree of error. This is a time to execute what's already been learned, not to work out new technique.
  • Higher pressure, higher stakes — closer to what they'll feel at a dual or tournament.
  • Feedback is immediate and direct since the priority is short-term performance.
  • Use this sparingly and intentionally — for example, the practice right before regionals or a big dual.

Summary: If we treat every practice like a performance practice, wrestlers never get the room to make mistakes and actually learn. If we treat every practice like a learning practice, they won't be ready for the pressure of competition. Know which one you're running, and coach it accordingly.

05

Giving Good Feedback

Constructive and Positive Feedback

  • Consistent positive feedback helps build a culture of psychological safety — an environment where wrestlers feel safe to take risks, be themselves, offer up new ideas, and make mistakes.
  • Giving short-term achievable goals improves self-efficacy and gives motivation.
  • Clearly point out improvements. Even if a wrestler isn’t improving in wins and losses, she is probably improving in something else, like her technique.

Learning Feedback

  • 1) Quiz them. Ask them how they could improve on something instead of just telling them. Making them think reinforces long-term learning.
  • 2) Summary feedback. Feedback provided after a series of no-feedback attempts. Wrestlers retain more with summary feedback — we don’t correct every rep, especially with new wrestlers. When the goal is long-term learning, they need a break in feedback in order to develop their own internal feedback.
  • 3) Bandwidth. Give feedback only when the wrestler reaches a certain degree of error. Let them make small mistakes without being overwhelmed with feedback.

Summary: During learning practices (most of them), allow some degree of error and let wrestlers make mistakes without providing feedback. Ask questions first before giving feedback.

“Your double leg set up is awesome. I can tell you’ve been practicing it. What do you think about the finish?”
“I don’t know…”
“Where should your head be when taking the shot?”
“Um… up as high as possible.”

Performance Feedback

  • 1) Immediate feedback. Tell them directly right away, which leads to more improvement in short-term performance.

Summary: During a performance practice — the day before regionals, for example — let them know right away with less degree of error. There’s no need to ask questions.

“The double leg set up and shot was great. Keep your head up on the finish.”

Elite Mindset Feedback

  • 1) Praise the process and effort (what they can control).
“Wow. Your double is explosive. I can tell you’ve drilled it.” — instead of, “Wow. You’re a gifted athlete.”
If she is a “gifted” athlete, maybe she’s actually not — maybe she is the athlete she is because she’s been working at becoming an athlete her whole life, and all that time and effort can be praised too.

Summary: Providing effort- and process-based feedback consistently can make a big impact.

07

Technique Philosophy

1) Be great at a few things rather than average (or worse) at a lot of things.

“Teach a lot of the little.” “Focus on a few things and do it well… develop an identity, and this is what we are going to be good at. If you’re a team that gets that identity, it grows confidence in your team.”

Our identity will be the priorities in the technique web, based on what works at the highest levels. For example, single legs, snapdowns, and re-attacks/shot defense are by far the most common ways girls score at state, national, and senior-level events.

80/20 principle: 80% of our technique success comes from mastering 20% of wrestling skills.

2) Master the basic skills.

Fundamentals create highlights — Amit Elor and Carley Ceshker are perfect examples of this.

“Why do you think I’m the best player in the world? Because I never ever get bored with the basics.” — Kobe Bryant

We don’t have to “go back to the basics” because we don’t stop doing them. Practices look pretty consistent each week — we work and develop common positions.

“I fear not the man who has practiced 10,000 kicks once, but I fear the man who has practiced one kick 10,000 times.” — Bruce Lee

3) Score points and have fun.

Neutral Priorities

  1. Find your tie and control it
  2. Attack and score with your primary offense (single or snapdown for most)
  3. Have elite defense and re-attack — refuse to be taken down
  4. Be dangerous by having a throw (headlock or honey badger for most)
  5. If you get underneath her, get out quickly

Bottom Priorities

  1. Seal or two-on-one and find one point
  2. Keep wrists clear
  3. Keep our hips/legs underneath us

Top Priorities

  1. Be a hammer and run a half or bar
  2. Secure a wrist and have hip pressure at the same time
  3. Put her weight on her hands

Being a hammer means:

  1. Run everything hard
  2. Keep breaking her down until she does not want to get up again
  3. We are not shy about picking top, because we have the mindset that we will pin her

Mindset Priorities

  1. Have an elite mindset with elite mindset talk and actions
  2. Focus on the moment and follow a pre-match routine
  3. Have a strategy/mindset during a match to handle adversity and know what they want their coaches to say from the corner
  4. Turn nerves into excitement
  5. Set clear goals, know why we want to achieve them, and post their goals where we will see them
  6. Respond positively to adversity
  7. Identify what habits should be stopped or continued in order to be successful
  8. Have a process-over-outcome mentality and focus on what they can control, rather than what they cannot control
  9. Understand a habit loop and how to replace a bad habit with a good habit
  10. Know tips and strategies for good time management and organization
